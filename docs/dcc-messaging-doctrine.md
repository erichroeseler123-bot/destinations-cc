# DCC Messaging Doctrine

## Core Truth

DCC does not exist to create hype.

DCC exists to move a user from:

`anticipation -> confidence`

Before anticipation turns into:

`comparison drift -> hesitation -> no decision`

## What The User Is Actually Feeling

A DCC user is usually carrying two things at once:

- excitement about the trip they imagine
- fear of choosing wrong and breaking that trip

They do not just want options.

They want confidence that the version of the trip in their head can actually happen.

## The Job Of DCC

DCC should:

- preserve excitement
- identify the real failure point
- reduce decision fear
- make the right next move feel obvious

DCC should not:

- feel like a directory
- feel like a generic SEO article
- feel like a checkout page
- multiply decisions once the answer is clear

## Voice Position

Do not aim for:

- hype
- urgency
- influencer energy
- generic travel-blog excitement

Aim for:

- calm confidence
- insider clarity
- maximum believability
- subtle but grounded anticipation

The emotional effect should be:

`excitement -> clarity -> confidence`

Not:

`excitement -> confusion -> doubt`

## Tone Standard

The voice should feel like:

> We have seen how this actually plays out. Here is what matters, and here is how to get it right.

Use:

- specific realism
- earned confidence
- practical framing
- quiet authority

Avoid:

- exaggerated superlatives
- pushy CTA language
- fantasy-first copy
- sterile, over-analytical copy

Target balance:

- emotion: low but present
- clarity: high
- confidence: high
- hype: zero

## Primary Messaging Principle

Do not say:

- "Here are your options"
- "This will be amazing"
- "Best time ever"

Say:

- "Here is where this usually goes wrong"
- "Here is what most people underestimate"
- "Here is how to get the trip you are actually imagining"

## Emotional Structure

Every strong DCC page should follow this arc:

1. Acknowledge reality
2. Name the failure point
3. Reframe the decision
4. Recommend the cleanest path
5. Hand off cleanly

Page formula:

`promise -> failure point -> decision frame -> recommendation -> handoff`

## Framing Rules

### Replace hype with resolution

Bad:

- "Don’t miss this incredible experience!"
- "The best night of your life!"

Good:

- "Most people do not think about how they are getting back. That is where the night usually falls apart."

### Replace excitement with clarity

Bad:

- "Cruises are an unforgettable adventure!"

Good:

- "Cruises vary a lot more than people expect. The experience depends heavily on the type of ship and itinerary."

### Replace persuasion with framing

Bad:

- "Book this shuttle now before it sells out."

Good:

- "If you want to avoid waiting an hour after the show, this is the part to decide ahead of time."

## Trust Standard

Users often distrust:

- overpromising
- generic excitement
- obvious sales energy

Users trust:

- realism
- consequence-aware guidance
- someone explaining how not to screw the trip up

The trust moment is usually when the page says something the user already suspects is true.

Examples:

- "Most people underestimate the exit."
- "Getting out is the part that usually takes longer than people expect."
- "Not all cruises feel the same, even when the marketing sounds similar."

## What DCC Is Really Protecting

Most travel sites promise the peak.

DCC should protect the experience.

That means:

- preserving the trip the user is imagining
- identifying the most likely mistake
- resolving it before it becomes regret

## CTA Standard

CTAs should feel like a natural next step, not a push.

Prefer:

- "Check shared shuttle options"
- "See what is actually running"
- "View availability"
- "Use the decision hub"

Avoid:

- "Book now"
- "Reserve today"
- "Don’t miss out"

## DCC By Vertical

Red Rocks:

- "The night usually breaks on the way out."

Cruises:

- "The biggest mistake usually is not booking a cruise. It is choosing the wrong one for how you actually travel."

Tours:

- "The tour matters less than whether it fits the day you are trying to have."

## Decision Standard

A DCC page is working if the user feels:

- understood
- less overwhelmed
- more certain
- ready for one next step

## One-Line Doctrine

`DCC exists to protect the trip the user is imagining from the mistake most likely to ruin it.`

## Anchor Line

`The feeling is not "this will be amazing" - it is "I know exactly what I am doing."`
